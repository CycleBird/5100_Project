Progress Report: Making Self-Play Reinforcement Learning Feasible for Gomoku via Candidate Move Restriction
Zengzheng Jiang, Peter Wei, Weizhi Du
due April 5, 2026

Project Overview

This project studies candidate move restriction’s influence on self-play reinforcement learning for Gomoku, seeking to make the learning process more efficient without compromising model performance. In board games with large action spaces, the agent must consider many unimportant moves and thereby waste computation. Reducing effective action space in an interpretable way may accelerate training, rendering reinforcement learning methods practical under limited computational resources. In our experiments, we are building an AlphaZero-based Gomoku system and investigating move restriction strategies, including local-neighborhood restriction, threat-aware restriction, and a hybrid approach. We will then compare these methods against the unrestricted baseline. We aim to show that these restriction methods preserve strong performance while improving training efficiency, and to explore other reinforcement learning problems with large action spaces in decision-making systems.

Work Completed So Far

We implemented and trained a baseline model on a 6x6 board with no move restriction. The current version includes a board-game engine, Monte Carlo Tree Search, and a policy-value neural network. During training, the model generates data through self-play techniques. We also did some data augmentation by rotation and flipping, and then stored them in the replay buffer. We updated the policy-value network with minibatch learning. The model is used to play against a pure MCTS algorithm, and the best-performing checkpoint has been saved. In addition to the core training pipeline, we also implemented a human-vs-AI interface, which allowed us to inspect whether the trained policy behaves reasonably in actual gameplay settings. 

We trained the baseline model on the 6x6 board, which demonstrated the feasibility of our approach. However, the action space is limited for pruning, which is needed for producing meaningful efficiency gains with candidate move restriction. In response, we initially attempted to scale the environment to a standard 15x15 Gomoku setting, but the training time was underestimated. We therefore expanded the board to 8x8, which also required substantial changes to the training framework. Even with this reduced configuration, the training process for the baseline still required approximately 48 hours. In particular, we adapted the model and training pipeline to handle the much larger board and expanded the search space. Despite these challenges, we completed this migration and extended the framework to support training on the 8x8 Gomoku board. This transition dramatically increased the size of the state-action space, adding complexity for the self-play search approach.

The baseline also verifies whether our implementation of AlphaZero training framework works correctly. We played with the baseline model using the human-vs-AI interface, and the model is very strong and cannot be easily overcome.

We also monitored that during the training process, the training speed is majorly limited by the Monte Carlo Tree Search opponent. Since it is based on deep first search, it cannot be accelerated by GPU parallel computing. We expect that our move restriction method can save a lot of computation resources from MCTS because it can reduce the search space exponentially.
Upcoming works
In the next phase, we will start working on the candidate move restriction method and integrate it into the action-selection pipeline. Specifically, the restriction methods will include:
restricting candidate moves to positions within a fixed distance from existing stones,
selecting moves that form or block critical patterns (e.g., open three, open four),
prioritizing moves suggested by the policy network (top-k filtering),
and filtering moves based on simple heuristic scoring functions.
We will evaluate each method along multiple dimensions, including:

Training efficiency (e.g., wall-clock time, number of simulations per move),
Convergence behavior (e.g., training loss, policy entropy, stability),
Final playing strength (e.g., win rate against baseline and pure MCTS).

If there is enough time, we plan to explore potential extensions of this approach, such as:

Dynamically adjusting restriction strength during different training stages,
Learning-based move restriction (e.g., meta-learning a lightweight model to predict candidate sets),
Applying similar ideas to other reinforcement learning problems with large discrete action spaces.

These experiments will help us better understand the trade-offs between efficiency and performance, and evaluate whether candidate move restriction can make self-play reinforcement learning more practical in complex decision-making environments.
